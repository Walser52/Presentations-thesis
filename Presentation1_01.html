Model Why it fails Valid Assumptions? Extension Results Error Reduction Supplementary

The standard approach to deconvolution is via the \(\nu-\nu^{1/2}\) model carried out on a cyclic voltammetry experiment using a planar electrode assuming semi-infinite planar diffusion.

The model encodes the current response as a function of the scan rate and the electrode potential.

\[ I(\nu, V) = \overbrace{k_1 \nu}^{Cap+Pseudo} + \overbrace{k_2\nu^{1/2}}^{Diffusive} +\epsilon \qquad(1)\]

This can then be linearized:

\[ \frac{I(\nu, V)}{\nu^{1/2}} = k_1 \nu^{1/2} + k_2 +\epsilon \qquad(2)\]

But how good does it fit the experimental data?

Attention must be paid to the underlying assumptions before applying the model.

Battery models commonly make assumptions known to be false, such as describing phase separating materials with Fickian diffusion.

â€“RB Smith

Non-linearities

Noise is not random

For reasons rooted in electrochemistry, we extend the model in the following way:

\[ I(\nu, V) = \overbrace{\beta_1 \nu}^{Cap+Pseudo} + \overbrace{\beta_2\nu^{1/2} + \beta_3 \nu^{3/2}}^{Diffusive} + \epsilon \qquad(3)\]

Dividing by \(\nu^{1/2}\) gives us a model that is quadratic in \(\nu^{1/2}\):

\[ \frac{I(\nu, V)}{\nu^{1/2}} = \beta_1 \nu^{1/2} + \beta_2 + \beta_3 \nu + \epsilon \qquad(4)\]

And it is this model that we will test.

Derivation

To rectify this situation we turn to the classic papers by (Reinmuth, 1962), and (Nicholson and Shain, 1964) and we see that for a semi-infinite linear diffusion reaction of the form \(O + ne \rightleftharpoons R\) with only O present initially, the diffusion limited current, \(i_D\) , has the general form:

\[ i_D = \Lambda \sigma^{1/2} \chi(\sigma t) \qquad(5)\]

where \(\Lambda\) and \(\sigma\) are:

\[\begin{align} \Lambda &= nFAC_O^*(\pi D_O)^{1/2}\\ \sigma &= \frac{nF\nu }{RT} \end{align}\]

The normalized current \(\chi\) can be given the series expansion:

\[\begin{align} \chi(\sigma t) &= \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} c_j exp{\Biggl[- \frac{jnF}{RT}(E-E_0) \Biggl]}\\ &= \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} c_j exp{\Biggl[- \frac{jnF}{RT}E + \frac{jnF}{RT}E_0 \Biggl]}\\ &= \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} c_j exp{\Biggl[-\frac{jnF}{RT}(E_i - \nu t) + \frac{jnF}{RT}E_0\Biggl]}\\ &= \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} c_j exp{\Biggl[-\frac{jnF}{RT}(E_i - E_0) \Biggl] exp [j\sigma t]}\\ &= \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} d_j exp[j \sigma t]\\ &= \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} d_j \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(j\sigma)^n}{n!} \label{eq-normcurrent} \end{align}\]

Where the \(c_j\) and \(d_j\) are expansion coefficients.

Therefore substituting the series expansion for the normalize current into Equation 5, we see that the diffusion limited current is a function of (2n+1)/2 powers of \(\nu\) where \(n \in \mathbb{N}\), the set of natural numbers. And the net diffusive current is then: : \[ i_D = \Lambda \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} d_j \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{j^n \sigma^{n+1/2}}{n!} \qquad(6)\]

We can now extend the model by adding a diffusive correction from the next order term and thereby get a model that is quadratic in : \[ I(\nu, V) = \overbrace{\beta_1 \nu}^{Cap+Pseudo} + \overbrace{\beta_2\nu^{1/2} + \beta_3 \nu^{3/2}}^{Diffusive} + \epsilon \qquad(7)\]

\[ \frac{I(\nu, V)}{\nu^{1/2}} = {\beta_1 \nu^{1/2}}+ \beta_2 + \beta_3 \nu + \epsilon \qquad(8)\]

where \(\beta_i\) are the regression parameters; \(I\), \(V\), and \(\nu\) have their usual meanings and \(\epsilon\) again is the noise term. Parameters \(\beta_2\) and \(\beta_3\) correspond to the diffusion limited Faradaic current but the first parameter, \(\beta_1\) would encompass the contribution from the Double Layer (DL) as well as the Faradaic, though non-diffusion limited contribution from pseudocapacitive effects.

Summary

The overarching objective was the evaluation of materials for energy storage.

The \(\nu-\nu^{1/2}\) was providing (in many cases) classifications that were totally incorrect.

This misclassification results in wrong materials being selected.

The quadratic model provides a more accurate deconvolution of the charge storage contributions.

Atomistic calculations are needed however to ask deeper questions. Such as how does one find candidates for energy storage so that they may be synthesized in the lab.

DFT calculations and cheminformatic analyses on MOFs help us identify design principles as they manifest themselves through trends in the data. For instance:

  • Row 3 and 4 transition metals are more conductive (d-orbitals)
  • Nd tends to outperform others
  • The heavily tested Zn yields insulating behavior
  • Smaller, non-aromatic linkers tend to perform better (at the expense of porosity)

We do this in the hope that it would not only accelerate the discovery of new materials for energy storage but also ensure their accurate classification.

One possible future direction could be to zoom in on the opportunities identified and examine them more closely.
